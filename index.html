reading you

a quick check-in

The moment the app opens, the front camera activates and the perception engine begins reading facial micro-expressions in real time. Subtle shifts in the brow and mouth, micro-movements around the eyes, all mapped against a running emotional baseline unique to each user. The entire process takes just a few moments from launch to reflection. No buttons, no prompts, no interaction required. The result is a single emotional snapshot that serves as the entry point for everything else in the app.

what it reads

The engine tracks forty-two facial landmarks across six primary expression zones. It measures both the static position of each feature and its rate of change over the reading window. Composite signals like tension gradients and bilateral symmetry are weighted into the final assessment. The system adapts its sensitivity to each user over time, accounting for natural resting expressions and individual variation. All of this runs through a lightweight on-device model with no cloud dependency.

your baseline, not theirs

Every reading is compared against the user's own historical data, not a population average. This means the face read becomes more accurate and more personal the longer someone uses it. A slight brow furrow might be unremarkable for one person and a significant shift for another. The system learns that distinction. There are no normative benchmarks and no comparison to anyone else.

privacy by design

Raw camera data never leaves the device. The perception engine processes the video feed locally, extracts the relevant landmarks, and discards the raw frames immediately. Only derived emotional scores are retained. The camera permission is a standalone toggle that can be revoked at any time without affecting other features. No biometric identifiers or facial geometry ever leave the phone.

your score

how it's calculated

Every day you get a single number measured entirely against your own history. No population benchmarks, no comparison, no ranking. It's a weighted composite of three pillars: groundedness (how centered versus reactive you are today), connection quality (the strength of your social and emotional interactions), and trajectory (your directional trend over the past seven days). Each pillar pulls from different parts of the app, from face reads to journal sentiment to how your interactions have been landing. The weighting adjusts dynamically based on which data streams have the most signal on a given day. If you skip the journal for a week, the score leans more heavily on face read and interaction data. The system never penalizes missing inputs.

personal baseline

The score is always relative to the user's own historical range. A 72 for one person might represent a strong recovery week, while the same number for someone else might signal a slow decline. The baseline recalibrates continuously using a rolling window of the last sixty days. Sudden life changes like a trip, an illness, or something unexpected are weighted differently to avoid distorting the trend. The goal is a number that feels honest, not one that feels good. It's a personal reflection tool, a daily touchpoint and a long-term record, not a clinical measurement or a replacement for professional support.

share cards

Each day generates a unique share card: your score over a color gradient that reflects the emotional arc of your day, paired with a single observation line. No two cards look the same because no two days feel the same. You can share cards directly with friends on miji or save them privately. The visual language is intentionally abstract so it communicates feeling without exposing specifics. Over time, a saved collection becomes a visual diary of how your weeks and months actually felt.

your people

relationship patterns

miji tracks how the user's emotional baseline shifts in proximity to specific people and interactions. A call with a particular friend might consistently correlate with a score lift, while a recurring meeting might reliably precede a dip. These patterns are surfaced over weeks and months, not from any single data point. The system never labels relationships as good or bad. It presents the data and lets the user draw their own conclusions.

sharing your day

You can share your daily card with anyone else on miji. The card shows the score and color gradient alongside an observation line, but nothing underneath. No journal content, no raw readings, no specifics. Recipients see how your day felt at a glance without needing a long conversation. It's designed for the kind of check-in that happens between close friends, partners, and family. Sharing is always opt-in, always one card at a time, and always revocable.

mutual visibility

When two users both share with each other, miji can surface connection-specific insights. Overlapping patterns become visible: days you both felt elevated, or stretches where one person's trajectory diverged from the other's. Both people choose to be here. Either one can step back at any time, no questions asked. It's designed for the kind of closeness where you actually want to know how the other person is doing.

friend groups

Small groups of miji users can form a shared circle where everyone's daily card appears in a single feed. The feed shows the day's color and score for each member. No journal content, no specifics, no comments. It is designed to feel like a quiet room where everyone's presence is visible but nobody is forced to speak. Groups are capped at twelve people to keep the signal meaningful.

what stays private

Everything personal stays with you. Journal entries, face reads, voice recordings, pillar breakdowns. The only thing anyone else can see is the composite score, the daily color, and that one observation line. There's no activity feed and no automated nudges when someone's score shifts. The boundary between personal data and shared data is clear, fixed, and controlled entirely by you.

your words

voice-first input

The journal is built around speaking, not typing. You talk naturally and your words appear as live transcription. Any phrase can be tapped to edit, delete, or expand on. The transcription engine runs on-device with no cloud processing of audio. This design choice exists because voice carries emotional data that typing does not, and the perception engine uses that signal to shape what happens next. The journal doesn't try to analyze or rewrite what you said. It just holds space. Takes in what you say, surfaces the signal, and keeps a faithful record. A mirror, not a therapist.

contextual prompts

Journal prompts are not pulled from a static library. They're generated from what the perception engine picks up during the session. Maybe a tightened voice around a certain topic, a trailing sentence, or a facial expression that doesn't quite match the words. The prompts stay gentle and open-ended. Never leading, never prescriptive. If you ignore one, it doesn't come back. The system follows your lead, not the other way around.

what surfaces

At the close of each journal session, the five highest-weighted context words are displayed in gold. These are the words the perception engine identified as carrying the most emotional significance during the session. Users see exactly which words are selected before anything leaves the device. Gold words are the only journal-derived data that contribute to the anonymous aggregate. They become small markers across weeks and months that trace the themes running through someone's life.

session history

Free users can access one journal session per day. Members get unlimited sessions with full history and search. Past entries are stored entirely on-device and can be browsed by date or by the gold words that surfaced in each session. The search function lets users find every session where a specific word or theme surfaced. Over months of use, the journal becomes a searchable emotional record that no other app or notebook can replicate.
