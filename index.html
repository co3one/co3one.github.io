the face read

three-second check-in

The moment the app opens, the front camera activates and the perception engine begins reading facial micro-expressions in real time. Subtle shifts around the brow, the corners of the mouth, and the muscles around the eyes are mapped against a running emotional baseline unique to each user. The entire process takes roughly three seconds from launch to reflection. No buttons, no prompts, no interaction required. The result is a single emotional snapshot that serves as the entry point for everything else in the app.

what it reads

The engine tracks forty-two facial landmarks across six primary expression zones. It measures both the static position of each feature and its rate of change over the reading window. Composite signals like tension gradients and bilateral symmetry are weighted into the final assessment. The system adapts its sensitivity to each user over time, accounting for natural resting expressions and individual variation. All of this runs through a lightweight on-device model with no cloud dependency.

your baseline, not theirs

Every reading is compared against the user's own historical data, not a population average. This means the face read becomes more accurate and more personal the longer someone uses it. A slight brow furrow might be unremarkable for one person and a significant shift for another. The system learns that distinction. There are no normative benchmarks and no comparison to anyone else.

privacy by design

Raw camera data never leaves the device. The perception engine processes the video feed locally, extracts the relevant landmarks, and discards the raw frames immediately. Only derived emotional scores are retained. The camera permission is a standalone toggle that can be revoked at any time without affecting other features. No facial geometry, no images, and no biometric identifiers are ever transmitted or stored outside the phone.

how it's calculated

The score is a weighted composite of three pillars: groundedness, connection quality, and seven-day trajectory. Each pillar draws from different data sources within the app, including face read results, journal sentiment, and interaction patterns. The weighting adjusts dynamically based on which data streams have the most signal for a given user on a given day. If someone skips the journal for a week, the score leans more heavily on face read and interaction data. The system never penalizes missing inputs.

personal baseline

The score is always relative to the user's own historical range. A 72 for one person might represent a strong recovery week, while the same number for someone else might signal a slow decline. The baseline recalibrates continuously using a rolling window of the last sixty days. Sudden life changes like travel, illness, or major events are weighted differently to avoid distorting the trend. The goal is a number that feels honest, not one that feels good.

share cards

Each day generates a unique share card with the score, a color gradient reflecting the day's emotional arc, and one observation line. No two cards look the same because no two days feel the same. Users can share cards directly with friends on miji or save them privately. The visual language is intentionally abstract so it communicates feeling without exposing specifics. Over time, a saved collection becomes a visual diary of how your weeks and months actually felt.

what the score is not

The miji score is not a mental health diagnosis, a clinical measurement, or a ranking system. It does not compare users to each other or to any external standard. It is not designed to replace professional support or therapeutic assessment. The score is a personal reflection tool that helps users notice patterns in their own emotional landscape. It is most useful as a daily touchpoint and a long-term record, not as a definitive verdict on any single day.

connections

relationship patterns

miji tracks how the user's emotional baseline shifts in proximity to specific people and interactions. A call with a particular friend might consistently correlate with a score lift, while a recurring meeting might reliably precede a dip. These patterns are surfaced over weeks and months, not from any single data point. The system never labels relationships as good or bad. It presents the data and lets the user draw their own conclusions.

sharing your day

Users can share their daily card with anyone else on miji. The card shows the score, the color gradient, and an observation line, but it does not expose journal content, raw readings, or specific triggers. Recipients see how the sender's day felt at a glance without needing a long conversation. It is designed for the kind of check-in that happens between close friends, partners, and family. Sharing is always opt-in, always one card at a time, and always revocable.

mutual visibility

When two users both share with each other, miji can surface connection-specific insights. Overlapping patterns become visible: days you both felt elevated, or stretches where one person's trajectory diverged from the other's. This is not surveillance or monitoring. Both users must actively opt in, and either person can revoke access at any time without notification. The feature is built for relationships where both people genuinely want to understand each other better.

friend groups

Small groups of miji users can form a shared circle where everyone's daily card appears in a single feed. The feed shows the day's color and score for each member. No journal content, no specifics, no comments. It is designed to feel like a quiet room where everyone's presence is visible but nobody is forced to speak. Groups are capped at twelve people to keep the signal meaningful.

what stays private

Journal entries, face read data, voice recordings, and individual pillar breakdowns are never shared. Only the composite score, the daily color, and the single observation line are visible to anyone outside the user's own view. There is no activity feed, no notifications when someone's score drops, and no system-generated prompts to reach out. The boundary between personal data and shared data is clear, fixed, and controlled entirely by the user.

the journal

voice-first input

The journal is built around speaking, not typing. Users talk naturally and their words appear as live transcription. Any phrase can be tapped to edit, delete, or expand on. The transcription engine runs on-device with no cloud processing of audio. This design choice exists because voice carries emotional data that typing does not, and the perception engine uses that signal to shape what happens next.

contextual prompts

Journal prompts are not pulled from a static library. They are generated from what the perception engine observes during the session: a tightened voice around a certain topic, a trailing sentence, a shift in cadence, or a facial expression that contradicts the words being spoken. The prompts are gentle and open-ended, never leading or prescriptive. If the user ignores a prompt, it does not reappear. The system follows the user's lead, not the other way around.

gold words

At the close of each journal session, the five highest-weighted context words are displayed in gold. These are the words the perception engine identified as carrying the most emotional significance during the session. Users see exactly which words are selected before anything leaves the device. Gold words are the only journal-derived data that contribute to the anonymous aggregate. They become small markers across weeks and months that trace the themes running through someone's life.

session history

Free users can access one journal session per day. Members get unlimited sessions with full history and search. Past entries are stored entirely on-device and can be browsed by date, by gold word, or by score range. The search function lets users find every session where a specific word or theme surfaced. Over months of use, the journal becomes a searchable emotional record that no other app or notebook can replicate.

what the journal does not do

The journal does not analyze, interpret, or diagnose. It does not offer advice, coping strategies, or therapeutic interventions. It does not generate summaries or rewrite what the user said. The role of the journal is to hold space: receive what someone says, surface the signal within it, and keep a faithful record. That restraint is intentional. The journal is a mirror, not a therapist.
